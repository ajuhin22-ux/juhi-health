3. Critical Finding #1: Hyperprolactinemia & Fertility Impact

Prolactin: 12.33 ng/mL (May 2025) → 40.4 ng/mL (March 2026)
Reference: 2.6-31.9 ng/mL (non-pregnant). A 3.3x increase in 10 months. This was NOT present at baseline.

Why This Is the Most Urgent Finding

Prolactin is the hormone responsible for breast milk production after delivery. In non-pregnant, non-lactating women, it should remain low. Elevated prolactin (hyperprolactinemia) has direct, well-documented effects on fertility:

MechanismEffect on Fertility
Suppresses GnRH pulsatilityReduces LH and FSH signaling to ovaries, potentially preventing follicle maturation and ovulation
Disrupts LH surgeWithout the mid-cycle LH surge, the dominant follicle may not release an egg (anovulation)
Shortens luteal phaseEven if ovulation occurs, the post-ovulation phase may be too short for implantation
Impairs progesterone productionInadequate progesterone means the uterine lining may not support embryo implantation
May cause subclinical anovulationPatient may still menstruate regularly but without actually ovulating - cycles appear normal but are infertile

Context: Combined Impact with Low AMH

With AMH at 0.80 (diminished ovarian reserve), the patient has fewer eggs than expected for age 31. If elevated prolactin is causing even occasional anovulation, this represents a significant loss of limited fertile cycles. The window for conception is narrow - every cycle counts. The urgency of treating this cannot be overstated.
